The humanitarian crisis raging in Yemen is considered the world’s worst. When conflict erupted in 2015, the country was already the poorest country in the Middle East; since then, Yemen’s health infrastructure and economy has been reduced to shambles, and its population dragged on the brink of famine.

“Under international humanitarian law” however, explains René Nijenhuis from OCHA, “the obligations of parties to the conflict are very, very clear: it is their obligation to protect humanitarian partners and the movements that we take”. Following a request of the Humanitarian Country Team, a Notification mechanism has been instituted in Yemen in April 2015.

The mechanism is run by OCHA’s Deconfliction Liaison Team (DLT). This unit serves as a conduit between UN humanitarian organisations and NGOs that wish to share their data (GPS coordinates and/or movement information), and the Evacuation and Humanitarian Operations Committee (EHOC) of the Saudi-Led Coalition.

The deconfliction may be temporary, for specific land, sea or air movements that humanitarians need to take; or permanent, for venues such as offices, residence, warehouses, hospitals, schools, refugee camps, water distribution sites, food distribution points etc.

The Notification mechanism does not constitute a legally binding agreement between any of the involved parties. As such, it can’t guarantee the safety of personnel, facilities, or sites - but it is still a valuable form of mitigation of risk for humanitarian operators.
